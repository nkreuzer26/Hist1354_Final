# Hist1354_Final
This project examines the economic and human development consequences of five US-backed military coups in Latin America during the Cold War: Chile (1973), Brazil (1964), Ecuador (1963), the Dominican Republic (1965), and Argentina (1976). Using the Synthetic Control Method, each country's post-intervention trajectory is compared against a synthetic counterfactual made from a weighted combination of countries that did not experience the intervention of interest. Two outcome variables are analyzed for each case: GDP per capita, from the Maddison Project Database, and the Augmented Human Development Index, which incorporates health, education, income, and civil and political freedom into a single composite measure.

The GDP per capita results vary across cases. Chile and Argentina show large and persistent negative gaps, with actual output falling well below the synthetic counterfactual and remaining there through the end of the study period. The Dominican Republic follows a similar pattern of sustained underperformance, though the concurrent US military occupation makes it difficult to attribute the effect to the coup alone. Ecuador displays a more gradual divergence that widens over time, suggesting compounding institutional damage instead of an immediate economic shock. Brazil is the outlier. After a brief post-coup dip, actual GDP rises above the synthetic from the early 1970s onward, reflecting the military government's aggressive state-led industrialization program, though whether this represents a genuine gain or growth borrowed through unsustainable debt remains unclear from the GDP data alone.

The AHDI results were much more consistent across countries. In all five cases, the index drops at the first observation following the intervention, driven primarily by the suppression of civil and political freedoms that GDP does not capture. This finding holds across countries with very different economic outcomes, suggesting that while the material consequences of military rule depend on the specific policies each regime adopted, the human development costs are more consistently negative. Taken together, the two sets of results indicate that evaluating military interventions through income alone understates their full cost, and that the suppression of political freedom represents a distinct and measurable dimension of harm that can persist for as long as authoritarian rule lasts.
